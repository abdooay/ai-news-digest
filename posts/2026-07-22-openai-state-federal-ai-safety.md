## What changed

OpenAI's Chief Global Affairs Officer, Chris Lehane, published an essay arguing that a US approach to regulating powerful ("frontier") AI models is taking shape through both state and federal action.

He points to California, New York, and Illinois as having passed frontier AI safety laws that share three core elements:
- A documented safety framework, with risk assessments for frontier models and public disclosure of the assessments and results.
- Required reporting of serious safety incidents.
- Governance and accountability through independent, outside audits.

OpenAI calls this pattern "reverse federalism" — states adopting similar laws creates a de facto national standard even without a single federal law. The essay argues California set the core disclosure framework, New York showed it could be adopted elsewhere, and Illinois added a requirement for independent verification.

At the federal level, the piece says the Trump Administration is working on a framework for US government testing of the most capable AI models on cyber risk, which OpenAI expects by early August, and that members of Congress from both parties (naming Reps. Jay Obernolte and Lori Trahan) have proposed federal frameworks. OpenAI's own "frontier safety blueprint" argues for: federal government leading testing and evaluation of the most advanced systems (built on the existing Center for AI Standards and Innovation, CAISI); clear requirements for companies building the most capable systems, including independent audits, incident reporting, security standards, and whistleblower protections; and state and federal rules reinforcing each other rather than conflicting. The essay also references discussion of a US-led international framework raised at a G7 meeting with Brazil, Egypt, India, Kenya, and Korea.

## Why it matters

This is OpenAI publicly stating its preferred shape for AI regulation, at a moment when several states have already passed relevant laws and Congress is actively drafting federal proposals. For any organization building on or deploying frontier AI models, the three elements OpenAI highlights — safety-framework disclosure, incident reporting, and independent audits — are a reasonable preview of what compliance obligations may look like if federal legislation follows the state pattern OpenAI is describing.

## How to use it

There's no product or setting here — this is a policy position, not a release. If your organization is tracking AI regulation:
- The California, New York, and Illinois frontier-AI safety laws are worth reviewing directly, since OpenAI expects federal rules to converge on similar elements.
- Federal cyber-testing framework details are expected around early August 2026, according to this essay — worth checking back on for anyone affected by government AI testing requirements.
