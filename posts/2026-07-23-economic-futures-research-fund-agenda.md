## What changed

On July 22, 2026, Anthropic published a detailed research agenda for its Economic Futures Research Fund, a $200 million program that pays for external research into how society can adapt to AI's economic effects. The stated goal is to make the economy "more flexible and resilient," make sure AI's benefits are shared broadly, and minimize harm from the transition.

The agenda lists five research priorities:

1. **Workplace-level AI integration** — field experiments on how a company's choices about rolling out AI affect whether workers keep building expertise, and how the resulting productivity gains get split between workers and employers.
2. **Transition support** — evaluations of retraining, job placement, and sector-to-sector transition programs, including combining income support with intensive help finding a new job.
3. **Income support modernization** — testing changes to unemployment insurance and longer-duration income pilots, aimed at situations where people are out of work for a long stretch because of AI.
4. **Worker wealth-building** — randomized controlled trials and pilot programs testing ways for workers to hold a financial stake in AI-driven growth (for example, capital accounts or equity-sharing), rather than only earning wages.
5. **Public investment evidence** — large-scale pilots that fund human-facing service jobs (the kind involving direct contact with people), measuring the effect on employment and wellbeing.

Grants range from $1 million to $30 million. Eligible applicants are accredited universities, research institutes, and nonprofits with experience running field experiments. Applications go through a request-for-proposals process described on Anthropic's site.

## Why it matters

This is Anthropic putting real money and structure behind a specific worry: that AI could disrupt jobs faster than existing safety nets (like unemployment insurance) can handle. Rather than funding AI capability research, this fund pays economists, labor researchers, and nonprofits to test practical interventions — things like new income-support designs or worker equity plans — before large-scale disruption happens. For researchers or policy organizations in this space, it's a large, well-funded, and reasonably specific source of grant money.

## How to use it

- Eligible organizations: accredited universities, research institutes, and nonprofits with field-experiment experience.
- Grant sizes: $1 million to $30 million.
- Apply through the request-for-proposals process described on the announcement page (see Source link below).
