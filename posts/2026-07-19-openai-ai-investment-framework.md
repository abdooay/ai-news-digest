## What changed

On July 14, 2026, OpenAI published guidance for company leaders on managing AI spending in what it calls "the agentic era" — a period where AI agents (AI systems that can carry out multi-step tasks on their own, rather than just answering one question at a time) are becoming common at work.

The article recommends treating AI investment like a portfolio: broad access for everyday productivity tasks, function-specific workflows for repeatable jobs, and a smaller number of bigger, strategic projects built around a company's own proprietary data.

It lays out practical steps: get clear visibility into who is using AI, on which products, and how much; fund workflows based on how mature they are, moving from exploration to validation to full production; judge total cost by the outcome reached, not just the price per token; and for priority workflows, track cost per accepted, correct outcome rather than a flat price.

## Why it matters

Many companies are spending more on AI tools without a clear way to measure whether that spending pays off. OpenAI's central point is that the cheapest model per token isn't necessarily the cheapest to use overall — a cheaper model that fails or needs retries can end up costing more once you count the extra work needed to fix or redo it. For teams evaluating or justifying AI budgets, this offers a concrete way to compare options: cost per accepted result, not the sticker price of a model.

## How to use it

- If you manage an AI budget or a team building agentic workflows, start with visibility: know which products, models, and how much capacity each team is actually using.
- Sort your AI use cases into a portfolio: everyday tools everyone uses, function-specific workflows, and a few high-value strategic projects.
- Move workflows through funding stages: exploration (test whether the model can do the task at all), validation (test it against real cases and a clear quality bar), then production funding (for the integration, monitoring, and change management needed to run it at scale).
- For any workflow you're serious about, measure cost per accepted outcome — for example, a support case actually resolved, or a code change that passes review — instead of just the per-token price of the model.
